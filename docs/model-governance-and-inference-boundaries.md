# Model Governance and Inference Boundaries

## Objective

This note defines what the repository is allowed to infer, what it should refuse to infer, and how model outputs must be labeled. The project studies Bay Area startup formation dynamics using public data, but the data architecture is asymmetric: county-level annual business applications are available, while employer-transition outcomes are observed directly only at broader levels in the assembled within the acquisition layer replication set. That asymmetry is manageable, but only if inference rules are explicit.

## Core principle

The repository should distinguish three classes of empirical objects:

1. **Directly observed quantities**: values that appear in the downloaded public files without additional modeling.
2. **Deterministic transformations**: totals, shares, growth rates, concentration metrics, or standardized panels obtained through arithmetic operations on directly observed quantities.
3. **Model-implied quantities**: county-level employer-formation proxies or survival proxies inferred by applying state-level transition signals to county-level application counts.

Only the first two classes should ever be described as “observed.” The third class must always be described as “estimated,” “implied,” “proxied,” or “bridge-model output.”

## Why the distinction matters

The Census Bureau is explicit that the BFS business formation series are forward-looking and that projected formations are generated by an econometric model that estimates the probability that a business application becomes an employer business [1][2]. At the same time, the BFS methodology notes that the data available nationally and by individual states are built from EIN applications and payroll-based employer transitions in the Business Register and Longitudinal Business Database [1]. The annual county applications file, by contrast, gives a geographically rich count of applications but not a county-level panel of observed employer transitions in the controlled replication environment. Therefore, a county-level employer-transition estimate for the Bay Area is necessarily a bridge model, not a direct observation.

## What the repository can infer credibly

### A. County-level startup initiation intensity

Using the annual county business applications workbook, the repository can measure:

- county-level levels of business applications;
- annual growth rates;
- county shares within the Bay Area aggregate;
- concentration measures such as the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index;
- application growth relative to pre-2020 or 2005 baselines.

These are deterministic transformations from observed county counts and are analytically strong.

### B. State-anchored employer conversion proxy

The repository can construct an annual bridge from California monthly BFS totals:

$$\widehat{E}_{c,t} = BA_{c,t} \times \theta_{CA,t}$$

where:

- $\widehat{E}_{c,t}$ is the estimated number of employer formations implied for county $c$ in year $t$,
- $BA_{c,t}$ is observed county business applications,
- $\theta_{CA,t}$ is a California-level conversion factor derived from the spliced four-quarter formation series relative to business applications.

A practical implementation uses:

$$\theta_{CA,t} = \frac{\overline{SBF4Q}_{CA,t}}{\overline{BA}_{CA,t}}$$

where the bars denote annual averages of monthly seasonally adjusted California series.

This is not a claim that all counties share identical transition mechanics. It is a structured approximation that imports the state-level conversion environment into county-level initiation counts.

### C. Concentration-adjusted regional exposure

The project can also estimate how much of Bay Area implied employer formation is concentrated in a small number of counties. This is analytically useful because capital, labor-market depth, permitting conditions, and founder networks are not evenly distributed across the region.

## What the repository should not infer without new data

The following claims exceed the evidentiary base of the core source architecture and should not be presented as established facts:

1. **Startup failure rates by county**  
   Business applications are not business deaths. A decline in applications is not a failure event.

2. **Company-level survival probabilities**  
   The public data architecture is aggregated, not firm-level.

3. **Causal effects of county or regional policy**  
   County-level trends may reflect macro conditions, industrial mix, labor demand, financing cycles, or regional spillovers. The available files do not isolate treatment effects.

4. **Sector-level Bay Area startup dynamics from the county workbook alone**  
   The annual county workbook contains annual application totals by county, not county-by-sector detail.

5. **Direct equivalence between BFS formations and BDS startups**  
   The BFS methodology itself states that BFS and BDS do not match exactly because the two systems identify startup events differently [1].

## Labeling standard for outputs

Every output should use one of the following labels:

- **Observed**: taken directly from a public file.
- **Derived**: arithmetic transformation of observed values.
- **Estimated proxy**: model-implied value obtained from a bridge, mapping, or external coefficient.
- **Coverage diagnostic**: statement about what is and is not present in the assembled replication set.

Example:
- “Observed Bay Area business applications in 2024: 97,505.”
- “Derived Bay Area annual decline in 2024 relative to 2023: -3.6%.”
- “Estimated proxy Bay Area employer formations in 2024 under the California spliced BF4Q bridge: approximately 8,141.”
- “Coverage diagnostic: Bay Area QCEW county files are not included in the acquisition layer.”

## Sensitivity design

All model-implied estimates should be accompanied by a sensitivity band. A practical approach is to compute:

- baseline conversion using annual average California SBF4Q / BA;
- lower bound using $\theta_{CA,t} - 1\sigma$ of monthly conversion ratios;
- upper bound using $\theta_{CA,t} + 1\sigma$.

This can be written as:

$$\widehat{E}^{L}_{c,t} = BA_{c,t}(\theta_{CA,t} - \sigma_{CA,t}), \quad
\widehat{E}^{U}_{c,t} = BA_{c,t}(\theta_{CA,t} + \sigma_{CA,t})$$

with the floor constrained at zero. The band is not a formal confidence interval; it is a disciplined stress range.

## Governance implications for downstream extensions

If later repository extensions add BDS county-level startup files, SUSB establishment counts, or venture-financing data, the governance rule should remain unchanged:

- observed counts remain primary;
- cross-system reconciliations are allowed but must be disclosed;
- model outputs never replace source data;
- naming conventions should preserve whether a variable is observed, derived, or proxied.

A clean naming grammar is recommended:

- `*_obs`
- `*_derived`
- `*_proxy`
- `*_coverage_flag`

## Practical inference hierarchy

For this repository, the preferred empirical hierarchy is:

1. County annual applications from the Census annual county workbook.
2. California monthly BFS application and spliced formation series.
3. Geographic delineation files for county membership in Bay Area statistical units.
4. QCEW and SUSB layers for later operating-context enrichment.
5. Optional external financing or patent layers only after the public federal foundation is stable.
